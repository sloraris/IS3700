Answers to questions you didn't ask

Why Bootstrap is the Worst

Bootstrap, the beloved Swiss Army knife of web development, beloved by some, tolerated by many, and the bane of the bespoke web artisan. It's like that one-size-fits-all hat: sure, it covers your head, but at what cost to style?
With its cookie-cutter templates and a grid system that’s about as subtle as a sledgehammer, Bootstrap has homogenized the internet into a flat, buttoned-up wasteland of sameness. Every site stands as a monument to the minimal effort of its creator—testament to the fact that in the kingdom of mediocrity, Bootstrap is king.
And let’s not forget the cascade of generic navbars and jumbo headers that scream 'creativity sold separately.' Dive into the Bootstrap soup, and you’re swimming in a sea of predictability, where the only surprise left is accidentally discovering a color that isn’t part of its standard palette.

Water isn't wet

In an unprecedented revelation, it turns out that water has been pulling the wool over our eyes for millennia—it isn’t wet. How can it be, when it has never known the parched, arid existence of dryness?
To say water is wet would be as misguided as claiming fire is burnt; a semantic impossibility and a wet blanket to the fluid truth. So next time you dive into a pool and feel that familiar sensation, remember: you're not actually wet, you're just less dry.
This brings us to ponder the real crises of our age—like if fish swim, do they also know the plight of walking? Or are they merely hostages to perpetual submersion, blissfully unaware of the world beyond the waves? Thus, the aquatic conspiracy deepens, sending ripples through our understanding of wetness.
We stand on the shore of discovery, gazing into the damp abyss, our minds soaked in wonder and confusion alike.

Sour cream isn't real

In the great dairy debate, one truth remains unchallenged: sour cream is not real cream, it’s just milk gone rogue. This dairy pretender has masqueraded as a condiment for far too long, sneaking onto baked potatoes and burritos under the guise of enhancing flavor.
In reality, it's nothing more than milk that didn't have the stamina to remain fresh. Sour cream is the culinary equivalent of failing upwards—celebrated for its tang, but it's really just the taste of defeat. Let's be honest, calling it 'sour' is just polite; it's the dairy world’s lemon: turned bad, but somehow we're all pretending it’s supposed to taste that way.
It's the little lie in every fridge, a reminder that not all that sours is lost, just transformed into a dip.
